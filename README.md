### The long version

I've been writing software long enough to have strong opinions —  
and humble enough to know when I'm wrong about them.

Over the years I've worked across the full stack: designed  
distributed systems from scratch, shipped consumer products  
used by millions, led engineering teams through rewrites  
that actually succeeded, and debugged production incidents  
at 2am more times than I'd like to count.

I don't have a favorite language. I have favorite **principles** —  
simplicity, observability, and relentless focus on the user.  
The language is just the medium.

Right now I'm most interested in the intersection of  
**developer tooling**, **systems performance**, and  
**building things that developers actually want to use**.

### What a decade in this industry taught me

```
The hard problems are rarely technical.
Premature abstraction kills more projects than premature optimization.
The best architecture is the one your team can reason about at 3am.
Write tests for the next person, not just for yourself.
Shipping imperfect beats perfecting what never ships.
The most important skill is knowing what to build next.
```

---

### How I work

I've been on both sides of the table — IC and lead, startup and scale-up.  
That experience shapes how I approach every problem:

- **I read the codebase before I touch it.** Understanding beats cleverness.
- **I write for the reader, not the runtime.** Code is communication.
- **I treat production with respect.** Every deploy is a promise to users.
- **I mentor because I was mentored.** The craft compounds through people.

### A few things I believe deeply

> **"Good software is an act of empathy."**  
> Every interface, every error message, every line of a stack trace  
> is a conversation with another human. Write accordingly.

> **"The goal is not to write code. The goal is to solve problems."**  
> Code is the side effect. Never forget what you're actually doing.

> **"Teams beat individuals, always."**  
> The best thing I ever built was an environment where other people  
> could do the best work of their lives.
